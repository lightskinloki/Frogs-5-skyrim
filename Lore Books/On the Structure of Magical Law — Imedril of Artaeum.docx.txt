PREFACE
This text was not written to teach you magic.
If you are reading it, you already know magic. You have spent years — most of you a decade or more — developing real facility with at least two schools. You have cast thousands of spells. You have felt Magicka move through you and shape itself into light, into force, into fire, into the compelled compliance of another creature's will. You know what you are doing.
What you do not know — what almost no practitioner at any level of skill has ever been taught to articulate — is why it works the way it does. Not in the shallow sense of "Destruction spells damage because they channel destructive force" — anyone who has passed their Apprentice examinations can recite that. I mean something more fundamental: the underlying grammar of magical expression. The structure that was always there, that every mage uses every time they cast anything, that almost no one has ever stopped to describe in terms precise enough to be genuinely useful.
That is what this text describes.
I want to be clear about what I have and have not done here. I did not discover the somatic components. They were not waiting to be found, like a vein of mineral in stone. They were always operating, in every spell ever cast, by every mage who ever lived. What I have done is name them. Describe them. Arrange them in a form that allows a mage who understands them to do something that most practitioners, even exceptional ones, cannot do: deliberately modify a spell's fundamental structure and predict what will change.
It is, I would argue, the difference between a mage who practices magic and a mage who understands it.
The components did not begin when I named them. They will continue long after this text is ash. I have only provided the vocabulary. What you build with it is your own work.
A note on audience: this text assumes expertise. It was written for practitioners with serious attainment in at least two schools. If you have not reached that point, you will find the component descriptions comprehensible but the practical applications largely inaccessible. The somatic components are comprehensible in the abstract. Using them requires the experiential foundation that only genuine mastery provides. Read it now if you wish. Return to it when you have done the work.
[ Archivist's Note — Telvani Orvil, Senior Scholar, College of Winterhold, 4E 63 ]

PART ONE: THE NATURE OF THE COMPONENTS
What a Somatic Component Is
Magicka does not respond to desire. This is the first thing to understand, and it is the thing that distinguishes a mage from someone who merely wishes very hard.
Magicka responds to relationship. When a mage casts a spell, they are not generating an effect from nothing — they are entering into a specific relationship with the laws that govern the behavior of the world. Magical law, like any law, has its own inclinations, its own resistances, its own conditions for compliance. A spell is not an imposition on reality. It is a negotiation with it.
The somatic components are the terms of that negotiation. There are twelve of them. Every spell ever cast, from the simplest cantrip to the most complex Master working, expresses some combination of these twelve relationships. The mage who casts Flames is saying, whether they know it or not: I am commanding fire to manifest. The mage who casts Healing is saying: I am petitioning the body to restore itself. The mage who casts Ironflesh is saying: I am anchoring myself against change, and I am binding that anchor in place, and I am stabilizing it against entropy.
They may never have thought about it in these terms. The spell works regardless. That is the nature of the components — they operate whether or not the caster understands them. But a caster who understands them can do something the unaware caster cannot: they can change which terms they are negotiating with, and thereby change the nature of what they are negotiating for.
Why This Matters
Consider what it means to swap a somatic component.
You know Fireball. You have cast it hundreds of times. You know its range, its area, its damage, the precise sensation of Magicka concentrating into that traveling point of potential and then releasing. You know it as a practitioner knows a tool they have used for years.
But Fireball, understood somatically, is a specific statement: COMMAND + BIND + PROJECT. You are forcing fire to manifest. You are binding it into a concentrated explosion. You are projecting it across distance. Change BIND to SHIFT — binding to fluid adaptation — and you have not cast a different spell. You have said a different sentence in the same language. The fire no longer concentrates into a single explosion; it arcs and adapts, chains between targets, seeks the path of least resistance like lightning rather than pooling like heat. You have modified the same underlying magical reality into a new configuration.
This is what this text teaches. Not new spells. A new relationship to the spells you already know — one that reveals them as expressions of a grammar you have been speaking fluently without knowing you could also compose.
A mage who cannot name what they are doing can only repeat it. A mage who can name it can change it.
The Twelve Components: A Complete Account
What follows is a description of each component — what it is, what it does to a spell, which schools employ it most naturally, and what it feels like in practice. I have tried to be precise without being reductive. These are not mechanical rules. They are descriptions of real relationships between a mage's will and magical law.
The school affinities noted for each component reflect where each relationship arises most naturally and intuitively. A mage trained in Destruction will find COMMAND effortless in ways a Restoration mage will not. This does not mean the Restoration mage cannot use COMMAND — it means they will have to work for it consciously rather than reaching for it automatically. All components are available to all mages. Fluency varies.






PETITION  —  Request the law to work as it naturally inclines
Of all the components, PETITION is perhaps the most misunderstood by those who have not studied it carefully. It is often described as "weak" or "passive" — as though making a request rather than a command reflects a deficiency of will. This is exactly wrong.
PETITION is the recognition that magical law has its own inclinations, and that working with those inclinations rather than against them is often more efficient, more precise, and more sustainable than forcing compliance. A Restoration mage who petitions the body to heal is not weaker than a mage who commands it — they are doing something more sophisticated. They are cooperating with processes that are already trying to occur. The body, given the chance, wants to heal. PETITION gives it that chance in an accelerated form.
In practice, PETITION spells feel cooperative. The mage extends their will not as a hammer but as an invitation. The result tends to be subtle rather than dramatic, controlled rather than volatile. PETITION spells are harder to resist for the recipient when the recipient is not actively opposing the effect, because you are not fighting their defenses — you are working alongside their own nature.
Where PETITION struggles: against targets that are genuinely opposed, genuinely unnatural, or genuinely inclined in the opposite direction. You cannot petition the dead to return to life. You cannot petition a hostile Atronach to calm itself. PETITION requires something to work with — some natural inclination that can be encouraged. When that inclination is absent, PETITION has nothing to request of.
Schools of natural affinity: Restoration, Illusion.


COMMAND  —  Force the law to work as you will it
COMMAND is the assertion of the mage's will over the natural inclinations of magical law. Where PETITION cooperates, COMMAND overrides. It is the most intuitive component for most beginning mages, because the fantasy of magic as power is fundamentally a fantasy of COMMAND — of forcing reality to comply.
COMMAND spells are dramatic. They are difficult to misinterpret. When you command fire to manifest, fire manifests with the weight of your authority behind it. The effect tends to be forceful, obvious, and resistant to subtlety. In situations where you need the maximum effect in the shortest time, nothing serves better than COMMAND.
The cost of COMMAND is the cost of all force: it provokes resistance. Targets of COMMAND spells feel the imposition of the mage's will and respond accordingly. Intelligent targets resist more effectively. The environment itself can resist — a COMMAND spell cast in a location saturated with competing magical authority may find itself partially negated. COMMAND also demands something of the caster: genuine certainty. A mage who wavers in COMMAND produces a wavering COMMAND, and a wavering COMMAND produces a spell that is simultaneously less effective and harder to control.
This last point deserves emphasis. Certainty is not confidence in the outcome — it is conviction in the act. A mage may be uncertain whether their Fireball will kill the enemy and still cast it with complete COMMAND. What they cannot do is cast it while uncertain whether they should. COMMAND spells feel the quality of the intention behind them.
Schools of natural affinity: Destruction, Conjuration.


ANCHOR  —  Ground yourself in unshakeable conviction
ANCHOR is the component of resistance. Where other components describe the mage's relationship to external magical law, ANCHOR describes the mage's relationship to themselves. To anchor is to plant — to make oneself a fixed point against which external forces push without result.
In practice, ANCHOR spells are tied to the caster's body and being in ways other spells are not. Flesh spells — Oakflesh, Stoneflesh, Ironflesh — are the clearest examples. The mage does not project protection outward; they become harder. The resistance is not a separate thing the mage carries. It is a quality of the mage themselves, in that moment.
ANCHOR spells resist interruption for the same reason. A spell that is anchored in the caster's conviction does not easily unravel when the caster is struck, distracted, or pressured. It is tied to something more stable than the moment of casting. A mage who has genuinely anchored a spell can maintain it through circumstances that would dispel a less-rooted working.
The limitation of ANCHOR is that what is anchored in the caster is limited to the caster. An ANCHOR spell extends only as far as the mage's own being reaches. You cannot anchor a protection on someone else as effectively as you can anchor it on yourself. You cannot anchor a spell that requires distance or separation from the caster — an ANCHOR and a PROJECT in the same spell exist in tension, and the results of that tension are always interesting.
Schools of natural affinity: Alteration, Restoration.


PROJECT  —  Extend your will across distance
PROJECT is the component of reach. Every spell that travels, strikes at a distance, or affects a target the caster cannot touch contains PROJECT. It is the bridge between the mage's will and a point in space that the mage does not physically occupy.
Understanding PROJECT clarifies why ranged spells behave the way they do. Reach costs coherence. The further a projected effect travels, the more it must maintain its own integrity against the entropy of distance. A COMMAND + PROJECT spell at extreme range is weaker than the same spell at close range, not because the mage has less power, but because the spell must sustain itself over greater distance. Project too far, and the spell disperses before it arrives.
PROJECT also naturally tends toward area. PROJECT creates the capacity for spread, though reach alone does not produce area coverage. A will extended across distance can be extended across a volume of space rather than a single point. Combined with the right secondary component, PROJECT becomes the engine of area coverage.
The mage using PROJECT should understand that they are doing something effortful in ways that close-range work is not. They are maintaining a connection between their will and a moving or distant point. Disruption of that connection — by the mage losing concentration, being struck, or casting in conditions of magical interference — is more likely to collapse a PROJECT spell than spells relying on other components.
Schools of natural affinity: Destruction, Conjuration, Illusion.


SHIFT  —  Allow the law to bend and flow
SHIFT is perhaps the most difficult component to explain to a mage who has not felt it, because it requires a disposition that runs counter to most magical training: willingness to let the spell do something you did not precisely specify.
All other components assert something about what the spell will do. SHIFT asserts only that the spell will adapt. A SHIFT spell finds the path of least resistance. It flows around obstacles rather than through them. It chains from target to target based on proximity and conductivity rather than the mage's direct intention. It changes form in response to the environment rather than imposing a predetermined form on it.
This makes SHIFT spells simultaneously powerful and unpredictable. Chain Lightning — COMMAND + PROJECT + SHIFT — is more dangerous to a group of enemies than a comparable single-target spell, but the mage cannot always predict exactly which targets will be struck, in what order, or how the arc will travel. The SHIFT component ensures the spell finds its own way, and its own way is not always the mage's preferred way.
Experienced mages learn to use SHIFT intentionally rather than tolerating it as a side effect. A mage who understands SHIFT can cast a SHIFT-containing spell in a configuration where the adaptation will produce the desired result — placing themselves so that the spell's natural path leads where they want it to go. This is a skill that takes time to develop. SHIFT is the component that most rewards experience and punishes the assumption that the spell will do exactly what you expect.
Schools of natural affinity: Alteration, Illusion.


BIND  —  Fix a moment so it cannot unravel
BIND is the component of persistence. Where RELEASE acknowledges the temporary nature of magical effects, BIND insists on duration. A BIND spell says: this change will not reverse easily. This effect will remain past the moment of its casting. Whatever I have done here, it will continue to be done.
In Conjuration, BIND is what distinguishes a summoned creature that serves from one that merely appears. An unbound summoning produces an entity that exists but owes no particular loyalty to the caster's intentions. A bound summoning produces an entity that is compelled — not by the caster's ongoing attention, but by a link that persists whether the caster is attending to it or not. This is both more reliable and more demanding: what is bound must be held, and holding has its own costs.
BIND in Alteration explains why flesh spells persist after casting — the change to the mage's physical substance has been fixed in place. BIND in Restoration explains why certain wards remain active through attacks that would dispel less anchored protections. In each case, BIND is the element that says the effect has been made into something more permanent than a passing intention.
The tension inherent in BIND: what is bound cannot easily be changed. A BIND spell is resistant to modification after casting — including modification by the caster. A mage who binds something into place and then wants to undo it has harder work ahead of them than a mage who chose RELEASE instead. BIND is the right choice when you want the effect to last. It is the wrong choice when you want the effect to be reversible.
Schools of natural affinity: Conjuration, Alteration, Restoration.


RELEASE  —  Surrender control and let the law reassert itself
RELEASE is the component that makes peace with impermanence. It is the mage stepping back. It is the acknowledgment that magical effects, by their nature, seek resolution — that reality tends toward its base state — and that a mage can work with this tendency rather than fighting it.
RELEASE spells are efficient. Because they are not trying to maintain themselves against the world's inclination to normalize, they cost less to sustain. They also dissipate naturally, without requiring any action from the mage to end them. For effects that the mage wants to be temporary, RELEASE is often the correct component: it makes the temporary nature of the effect part of the spell's design rather than a limitation to be worked around.
In Destruction, RELEASE appears in explosive effects that release their force all at once rather than sustaining it. The classic fireball does not need to maintain itself — it releases its energy in a moment and is done. In Illusion, RELEASE appears in effects that fade naturally as they are no longer needed, or that the mage wants to be able to end cleanly without residue.
RELEASE is not weakness. It is precision about what one wants. A mage who chooses RELEASE understands that the goal is the moment of the effect, not its duration, and accepts the tradeoffs that come with that understanding. RELEASE spells are fragile in ways that BIND spells are not. They can be disrupted, broken, or ended by external forces more easily than a bound effect. This is the cost of their efficiency.
Schools of natural affinity: Destruction, Illusion.


STABILIZE  —  Entropy is slow; this state is permanent
Where BIND makes an effect persistent, STABILIZE makes it resistant. The distinction is important. A BIND spell will last — but it can eventually be overcome, dispelled, or worn down. A STABILIZE spell insists that the current state is not just fixed but correct — that entropy itself will not erode it, that dispelling attempts will find nothing loose to pull on.
STABILIZE is the most demanding component to maintain. It requires the mage to continually assert that the stabilized state is the true state. This is why STABILIZE spells require concentration. The mage is casting and then holding, for as long as the spell persists, a kind of ongoing philosophical argument with reality that the current configuration is correct and should remain.
When that argument succeeds, STABILIZE spells are among the most powerful effects a mage can produce. Flesh spells with STABILIZE cannot be dispelled while the mage concentrates. Paralysis with STABILIZE resists every attempt at recovery. The downside is the cost of that concentration: a mage maintaining a STABILIZE effect is spending cognitive and magical resources continuously, and a single disruption — a significant blow, a failure of will — can collapse the entire thing.
STABILIZE is the component that most separates capable mages from exceptional ones. Beginners find it intellectually comprehensible and practically impossible. The ability to maintain genuine STABILIZE requires not just the technical capacity but the sustained conviction that the stabilized state is correct, throughout whatever the battle or situation demands. Many mages who can cast STABILIZE spells successfully in practice conditions find them extremely difficult to hold under genuine duress.
Schools of natural affinity: Alteration, Restoration.


ATTUNE  —  Become one with the law's expression
ATTUNE is unlike any other component in one essential respect: it does not describe what the mage does to magical law. It describes what the mage becomes in relation to it.
Every other component asserts a relationship. PETITION asks. COMMAND forces. BIND holds. SHIFT adapts. ATTUNE does none of these things. ATTUNE says: I am not separate from this magic. I am its voice. I am the place where this law is currently expressing itself, and in being that, I perceive things that an external observer cannot.
In practice, ATTUNE spells reveal secondary phenomena. Detect Life works because the mage attunes to the Magicka of living things and becomes momentarily permeable to its signal — they become briefly capable of feeling what is already present. Telekinesis contains ATTUNE because the mage who moves an object at a distance is not simply projecting force — they are becoming, for the duration, partially continuous with the object's position in space.
ATTUNE is available to all schools equally. It describes a mode of relationship to magic itself, prior to any particular operation on magical law. Mages who have developed ATTUNE capability often describe a change in how magic feels to them generally, not just in ATTUNE spells. A degree of permeability to Magicka becomes, with practice, a persistent quality rather than a deliberate choice.
Schools of natural affinity: All equally.


SEVER  —  Break connections and shatter bonds
SEVER is the component of separation. Where BIND creates connections and holds them, SEVER cuts them. Where ANCHOR roots things in place, SEVER removes what roots them. SEVER is the sharpness in magic — the edge that cuts rather than holds.
In Destruction, SEVER appears wherever the intent is to disrupt existing magical effects, to break hostile enchantments, to cut through the bindings that hold conjured creatures to their masters. Soul Trap contains SEVER because the soul must be severed from its natural transit path before it can be redirected into a gem.
This last example deserves particular attention, because it illustrates something important about SEVER: it does not discriminate. SEVER cuts what can be cut. In Soul Trap, SEVER cuts the soul's connection to its post-death destination — a clean, precise, devastating action that accomplishes exactly what the spell requires. The moral weight of that action is real and belongs to the mage. SEVER is a tool, and the things it can be used to sever include things that arguably should not be severed.
In application, SEVER is most useful against targets that have established magical connections: summoned entities, enchanted objects, warded enemies, mages actively maintaining spells. Against targets with no such connections, SEVER has less to work with. A SEVER component in a spell cast at an enemy with no active magic is not wasted — it still carries the destructive quality of separation — but it achieves less than it would against a well-connected target.
Schools of natural affinity: Destruction, Conjuration.


CONCEAL  —  Happen where no one is looking
CONCEAL is the component of invisibility in the broadest sense — visual, auditory, and attentional. A CONCEAL spell does not necessarily hide itself from all perception. It hides itself from the perception that matters in the current moment.
CONCEAL spells extend beyond being unseen. A Muffle spell does not make the mage invisible — it conceals their sound. An Invisibility spell conceals their form. Both use CONCEAL because they are doing the same fundamental thing: operating where a particular kind of attention is not looking. The component describes a relationship to awareness, not to any specific sense.
CONCEAL is the component of patience and timing. A CONCEAL spell cast at the wrong moment — when someone is already looking directly at you — fails not because the magic is wrong but because CONCEAL cannot manufacture inattention that does not already exist. It finds and occupies gaps in awareness. If there are no gaps, it has nothing to work with.
This is also why CONCEAL is difficult to maintain under direct assault. Combat tends to be the situation of maximum mutual attention. CONCEAL persists in combat not because the mage has hidden from the battle but because they have found the gaps — the moments between one enemy's attention and another's, the brief redirections that even a focused combatant experiences. Holding CONCEAL in a fight is not a passive process. It is the continuous exploitation of a resource that combat continuously tries to eliminate.
Schools of natural affinity: Illusion, Conjuration.


REVEAL  —  All hidden things are shown
REVEAL is the opposite of CONCEAL not in structure but in orientation. Where CONCEAL seeks gaps in attention, REVEAL seeks things that are using those gaps. It is the component of finding, exposing, and making manifest what would prefer to remain unmanifest.
Turn Undead uses REVEAL because the undead occupy a kind of magical concealment within the ordinary texture of the world — they are present, but the living senses do not register them as the threat they are. REVEAL strips away that assumption of normalcy, making the mage aware of what is present as a fact of magical law rather than background scenery.
In Restoration, REVEAL appears because healing — genuine healing — the correction of what is actually wrong, rather than the suppression of its effects — requires the mage to perceive what is actually wrong. REVEAL is how a skilled Restoration mage sees a disease rather than its surface effects, finds the site of a poison's action rather than simply its general debilitating influence. The better the mage's command of REVEAL, the more specific and effective their subsequent PETITION can be.
REVEAL spells are difficult to be subtle about. What is made manifest tends to draw attention. A REVEAL spell that exposes a hidden creature exposes it to everyone who can see, not just the mage. This is often exactly what is wanted. There are circumstances where it is not, and mages who rely on REVEAL for tactical purposes should develop the habit of considering what their revelation will reveal to.
Schools of natural affinity: Restoration, Illusion.

PART TWO: THE STRUCTURE OF SPELLS
How Spells Are Composed
Every spell is a sentence. The mage is the subject. The somatic components are the verbs. The target is the object. A spell with a single component is a simple sentence — direct, clear, limited in what it can express. A spell with five components is a complex sentence — capable of expressing relationships, conditions, and simultaneous actions that a simple sentence cannot approach.
The complexity of a spell's somatic structure corresponds to the skill required to cast it. Novice practitioners cannot hold more than a single component in their magical expression at once. It is the appropriate limitation of a developing practice. As a mage's skill and understanding deepen, they develop the capacity to hold multiple somatic relationships simultaneously, each one coherent, each one contributing its specific quality to the whole.
The tiers of magical practice correspond directly to the number of components a mage can coherently express:


SPELL TIER AND COMPONENT COUNT
  Novice     —  1 component
  Apprentice —  2 components
  Adept      —  3 components
  Expert     —  4 components
  Master     —  5 components


It describes the practical capacity that develops through sustained practice with a school of magic. A mage who has worked seriously with Destruction for years develops an intuitive fluency with COMMAND and PROJECT that allows them to combine those components without conscious effort. The second component requires no effort because the first has become automatic. This frees attention for the second. The same process, extended through Adept, Expert, and Master levels of practice, explains why experienced mages can do things that feel impossible to beginners.
School Affinities and What They Mean
Each school of magic has components it employs naturally — components that arise so often in that school's practice that a mage trained in the school develops an intuitive relationship with them without ever having studied the components explicitly.
A Destruction mage who has never read this text still knows, in their hands and their will, what COMMAND feels like. They have cast it thousands of times. They have felt its weight, its resistance, its tendency to produce dramatic results. If you tell them the word — COMMAND — they will recognize it immediately as describing something real that they already do.
This is the practical meaning of school affinity. It is not that other components are unavailable to a school — it is that the natural components feel like home, and the others feel like working in a foreign language. Fluent, in time. Not fluent yet.


SCHOOL NATURAL AFFINITIES
  Destruction  —  COMMAND, PROJECT, SEVER, RELEASE
  Restoration  —  PETITION, ANCHOR, STABILIZE, REVEAL
  Alteration   —  ANCHOR, SHIFT, BIND, STABILIZE
  Conjuration  —  COMMAND, BIND, PROJECT, ATTUNE
  Illusion     —  SHIFT, CONCEAL, REVEAL, ATTUNE, RELEASE


A mage swapping to a non-native component does something real and worth acknowledging. A Restoration mage who uses COMMAND in Healing Hands is not doing anything forbidden — but they are forcing the body to heal rather than asking it. That is a different thing. The body still heals. The experience for the recipient is different. The moral register of the act is different. Whether those differences matter depends on the situation and the mage's judgment. I note them because they are real, not because they are prohibitions.

PART THREE: THE PRACTICE OF MODIFICATION
What Modification Is
A mage who understands the somatic structure of their spells can modify them. The components have always existed in these combinations — every combination of components that makes logical sense has probably been expressed by some mage at some point in the long history of magical practice. What the mage is doing is choosing, deliberately, to express a combination they know but have not standardly used.
The modification is real. The modified spell is a real spell. Its effects are predictable from the components the mage has chosen. A mage who replaces BIND with SHIFT in Fireball will produce a fire effect that arcs and chains rather than pooling in an explosion, because that is what SHIFT does — it makes effects adaptive and fluid. That is what the components mean.
This is why the earlier portions of this text matter. A mage who has memorized the component list as a catalogue of rules can swap components mechanically. A mage who genuinely understands what each component is — who can feel the difference between BIND and RELEASE in their own practice, who knows what SHIFT does to a spell not because they were told but because they have felt it — that mage can modify spells and know what they have created.
The difference between understanding and memorization becomes most clear at the moment of modification. One produces a mage who can describe their creation. The other produces a mage who has to ask.
The Capacity for Modification
Modification becomes available as a mage's practice deepens — a description of how the capacity actually develops, not a gate imposed from outside.
A mage at the beginning of their serious practice can cast spells. They cannot perceive the somatic structure underlying what they are casting any more than a new reader can perceive the grammatical structure of a sentence they are reading. They comprehend the meaning — the spell works — without access to the mechanics that produce it.
As mastery deepens, the structure becomes perceptible. The experienced mage begins to feel, in the act of casting, what each component is contributing. This perception does not immediately translate into the ability to change the components — understanding that a spell contains COMMAND + PROJECT does not mean one can immediately swap PROJECT for BIND. But it is the necessary precondition for that ability.
Genuine modification — the deliberate, successful substitution of one component for another — typically becomes available to mages at the Expert level of practice in at least one school. At this level, the mage has sufficient intuitive fluency with the relevant components that they can attempt a substitution and have a reasonable expectation of success.
At the Master level, successful modification becomes reliable. The mage who has achieved genuine mastery of a school can modify any spell in that school without the uncertainty that attends a first attempt. The modification may be unusual. It may produce unexpected secondary effects. It will not fizzle.


MODIFICATION BY RANK
  Rank 1 — Apprentice  :  Cannot perceive or modify components. Cast as baseline.
  Rank 2 — Adept       :  Can perceive components. Understands why a spell works.
                          Cannot yet modify.
  Rank 3 — Expert      :  May attempt one component swap per spell.
                          Roll against your Magic skill.
                          Success: the modified spell works as intended.
                          Failure: the spell fizzles. You take 3 points of strain damage.
  Rank 4 — Master      :  Modifications are reliable. No roll required.
                          Declare the swap. Describe the result. Cast.


The Rules of Modification
Modification is not unconstrained. It operates within limits that are not arbitrary restrictions but logical consequences of what the components are. The following three laws describe those consequences.
The Law of Dilution
A component swap that transforms a single-target effect into an area effect reduces the effect's intensity by half. You cannot gain coverage without losing concentration. It is a description of what area coverage costs. The same quantity of magical force that strikes one target with full intensity strikes ten targets with a tenth of that intensity each, or a region of space with diminished density throughout. Spreading a spell does not multiply its power; it distributes a fixed power across a larger volume.


LAW OF DILUTION
  Single-target → Area of Effect: damage is halved.
  You gain coverage. You lose concentration.
  This applies to the modified variant only — not the baseline spell.


The Law of Sustain
A component swap that converts an instantaneous or fixed-duration effect into a concentration or permanent effect requires ongoing investment from the caster. A spell that is being held in place by the mage's active will — rather than completing itself and releasing — is a spell that continues to demand that will. Every turn the mage is maintaining such a spell, they are spending their Major Action to do so. If the mage is struck while concentrating, they must make a Magic roll or lose the spell entirely.
It is a description of what concentration costs. The mage who can hold a STABILIZE effect through a difficult battle is doing something genuinely impressive. The mage who cannot should understand why, and train accordingly.


LAW OF SUSTAIN
  Concentration / Permanent effects: costs a Major Action each turn to maintain.
  On taking damage while concentrating: make a Magic roll or lose the spell.
  The spell holds until the roll fails — then it ends.


The Law of Conservation
A modification cannot replicate the exact effect of a higher-tier spell. The components of a spell interact with its tier in ways that constrain what modifications can produce. A mage who swaps components in Firebolt cannot produce Fireball — not because of a rule against it, but because the component configuration of a two-component spell cannot produce the effect of a three-component spell. The interaction of COMMAND + BIND + PROJECT in Fireball produces something that cannot be approximated by two components, no matter which two.
What this means in practice: modifications produce variants, not upgrades. A modified Firebolt is a different Firebolt — one with different behavioral properties, different applications, different strengths and limitations. It is not a better Firebolt. If a mage wants Fireball, they need to learn Fireball.


LAW OF CONSERVATION
  A modified spell cannot replicate the exact effect of a higher-tier spell.
  Modifications produce variants — different behaviors, not more power.
  If you want the higher-tier spell, learn the higher-tier spell.


On the Limits of Modification
Not every combination of components is coherent. The components describe real relationships between a mage's will and magical law, and some relationships are mutually exclusive, some are ontologically impossible, and some simply have no meaningful interpretation. The mage who attempts an incoherent combination does not produce a strange result. They produce nothing. The magic will not cooperate with a sentence that contradicts itself.
The clearest examples are direct contradictions. REVEAL and CONCEAL cannot coexist in a single spell — one makes things manifest, the other makes them absent, and a spell cannot simultaneously do both. COMMAND and PETITION cannot coexist — you cannot simultaneously force and ask. Attempting either combination produces a structure that collapses under its own internal opposition before it can be expressed.
A second category of impossibility is ontological rather than structural. PETITION requires something to petition — some natural inclination that can be encouraged. Applied to the dead, it finds nothing. The dead have no inclination toward anything. SEVER requires connections to cut. Applied to a target with no active magical bonds, no conjured links, no sustained effects — it has nothing to work with. These are not forbidden combinations. They are simply empty ones. The component looks for what it needs and does not find it.
Beyond these hard limits, there is a broader question of coherence that each mage must answer for themselves. Before attempting any modification, ask: can I describe what this spell is doing in a single plain sentence, using the actual meaning of each component I have chosen? Not a vague gesture at an outcome — a precise description of the relationship being expressed. If the answer is yes, the modification is likely coherent, and the attempt is worth making. If the answer is no — if the mage finds themselves unable to explain the combination without resorting to desired outcome rather than component logic — they have not yet understood what they are trying to do. The spell will know.
I will note one further category worth attention: modifications that are valid but that cross a school's natural register. A Restoration mage who uses COMMAND in a healing spell is doing something real. The body will heal. But the experience is different — for the mage and for the recipient. The mage is forcing rather than asking, which is not how Restoration magic naturally moves, and the body knows it. It is a choice with consequences that the mage should understand before making it. Magic practiced against its own grain is still magic. It simply costs more, in ways that are not always obvious until they accumulate.

PART FOUR: WORKED EXAMPLES OF MODIFICATION
I have chosen five spells to work through in detail. They represent different schools, different component structures, and different kinds of modification — which is to say, different kinds of question about what a spell can become when one of its terms changes. I have not tried to be exhaustive. The possible modifications of any given spell are numerous, and cataloguing all of them would be tedious for both of us. What I have tried to do is choose variations that illuminate something real about how the relevant components behave — cases where the result follows clearly from the components involved, and where understanding why produces something more useful than simply knowing what.
A mage who can read these examples and understand why each modification produces the result described — not because they were told, but because they can derive it from the component definitions — has understood this text.


Fireball  ·  Adept  ·  COMMAND + BIND + PROJECT
In its standard form: You force fire to bind in a concentrated explosion at a point up to 120 feet away. 20-foot radius. Instantaneous. The fire pools and bursts from a fixed point.


Variation I — Replace PROJECT with RELEASE  COMMAND + BIND + RELEASE
You force fire to bind in an explosion that releases itself immediately. The explosion has no reach — it detonates at your feet or at close range. The fire burns out the moment it releases, leaving no residue.
Shorter range (near melee distance), no lingering flames. Easier to control and less chaotic — the RELEASE component accepts the spell's end rather than projecting it outward.
Variation II — Replace COMMAND with PETITION  PETITION + BIND + PROJECT
You ask fire to manifest as an explosion at a distance. The fire responds cooperatively rather than being forced into a shape. The explosion expands gently enough that allies in the area have a moment to react — they gain advantage on saving throws to avoid the effect.
Safer for allies. Less forceful against resistant or hostile targets. The fire cooperates; it does not impose.
Variation III — Replace BIND with SHIFT  COMMAND + SHIFT + PROJECT
You force fire to arc and adapt across distance rather than pooling in a single explosion. The fire chains between targets, finding paths of conductivity and proximity. Affects a 40-foot line rather than a radius sphere.
Greater lateral spread. Less concentrated at any single point — Law of Dilution applies if this creates area coverage beyond the original spell. Cannot be aimed with precision; SHIFT finds its own path.
Variation IV — Replace PROJECT with STABILIZE  COMMAND + BIND + STABILIZE
You force fire to remain in place as a persistent burning cloud. The explosion does not travel — it exists where you create it and stays there, requiring your concentration to maintain. Burning cloud, 20-foot radius, up to 10 minutes concentration.
No range (the fire exists at a location you must designate at casting). Requires concentration — Law of Sustain applies. Powerful area denial if the concentration can be held.


Paralyze  ·  Expert  ·  COMMAND + BIND + PROJECT + STABILIZE
In its standard form: You force the target's body to bind itself immobile at distance. Duration: 3 rounds. Target cannot move or take physical actions. Range: 60 feet.


Variation I — Replace COMMAND with PETITION  PETITION + RELEASE + ANCHOR
You ask the target's body to still itself. The body cooperates to the degree it is inclined to — which, in a hostile target, is not very much. Duration reduced to 1 round. Target has advantage on saving throws to resist.
Much weaker against unwilling targets. More effective against targets whose bodies are genuinely inclined toward stillness (exhausted, injured, or already partially immobilized targets).
Variation II — Replace BIND with ANCHOR  COMMAND + ANCHOR
You force the target to be anchored to their current location. The target is rooted in place — they cannot move from their position — but they can still act, cast, and fight. Duration: 4 rounds.
Target can still attack and cast spells. Effective for preventing retreat or repositioning. Weaker against targets who are comfortable fighting from a fixed position.
Variation III — Replace BIND with STABILIZE  COMMAND + STABILIZE
You force paralysis to persist beyond its natural duration. The paralysis resists all recovery and is extremely difficult to dispel. Duration: concentration, up to 10 minutes.
Law of Sustain applies — costs a Major Action each turn to maintain. Resists dispelling while concentration holds.


Soul Trap  ·  Apprentice  ·  COMMAND + SEVER + BIND
In its standard form: You force the soul to be severed from its natural post-death transit and bound to the soul gem in your hand. The link is established for 60 seconds. If the target dies while linked, their soul is captured. Single target. Requires a compatible gem held in the casting hand.


Variation I — Replace SEVER with CONCEAL  COMMAND + CONCEAL + BIND
You force the soul toward the gem while concealing the act from the target's awareness. The soul is drawn quietly rather than forcibly severed. The target does not perceive the trap being established.
The target (if intelligent) has no magical sense that Soul Trap has been cast on them. Mechanically, this primarily matters for intelligent or magically sensitive targets who might otherwise react to the established link.
Variation II — Replace BIND with STABILIZE  COMMAND + SEVER + STABILIZE
You force the severance to persist and resist reversal. The severed soul-transit cannot be restored even if the spell is detected and countered — the cut has been made permanent. Duration of the trap extends significantly.
Law of Sustain applies — costs a Major Action each turn to hold the severance. The soul's path to its natural destination is severed for as long as concentration holds, not just for 60 seconds.
Variation III — Replace COMMAND with PETITION  PETITION + SEVER + BIND
You ask the soul to sever its own transit and enter the gem. This is thematically significant: you are requesting cooperation rather than compelling it. Against unwilling souls (sapient beings), this is substantially less effective. Against animals and creatures without strong destination-inclination, the practical difference is minimal.
Against white souls: nearly equivalent to baseline. Against black souls: much harder to establish — the soul has a strong existing destination and must choose to cooperate with severance.


Invisibility  ·  Adept  ·  CONCEAL + SHIFT + RELEASE
In its standard form: You conceal yourself from sight, shifting the concealment with your movement, and allow it to fade naturally. Duration: 3 rounds or until you take a hostile action. Self only.


Variation I — Replace RELEASE with BIND  CONCEAL + BIND
You hide from sight and lock the concealment in place. The invisibility does not fade naturally — it must be broken by a hostile action or by your deliberate choice to end it. Duration: 30 minutes.
Harder to break accidentally. Also harder to end intentionally if needed. The binding cuts both ways — you cannot RELEASE yourself from it without effort.
Variation II — Replace RELEASE with STABILIZE  CONCEAL + STABILIZE
You anchor the concealment as a near-permanent state. Duration: 1 hour, concentration required. The concealment persists through disturbances that would normally break lesser invisibility.
Law of Sustain applies. Extremely long duration. Difficult to detect. The price of turning RELEASE into STABILIZE is that you must actively insist, every turn, that the concealment is correct.
Variation III — Replace RELEASE with ANCHOR  CONCEAL + ANCHOR
You hide from sight and root the concealment in your own conviction rather than letting it fade or binding it externally. The concealment is tied to your stability — while you remain physically still and mentally present, it holds with unusual strength. If you are struck or startled, it becomes harder to maintain.
Strong concealment while stationary. More vulnerable during movement or under assault than BIND or STABILIZE versions. Interesting for infiltration scenarios where stillness is possible.


Healing Hands  ·  Adept  ·  PETITION + BIND + ANCHOR
In its standard form: You channel a sustained stream of healing into an ally you are touching, asking the body to heal and binding the restoration in place. Restores 12 HP per turn while maintained. Requires physical contact.


Variation I — Replace PETITION with COMMAND  COMMAND
You force the body to heal itself — the BIND holds the restoration in place and the ANCHOR ensures it takes root. Heals 15 HP. The body is compelled rather than assisted, which the recipient feels as a forceful internal pressure. On unwilling targets this distinction is meaningful; on willing ones it is merely unpleasant.
Stronger healing. Thematically forceful — more appropriate in emergencies than in careful medical treatment. A Restoration mage who habitually uses COMMAND rather than PETITION is doing something their school would note.
Variation II — Replace BIND with RELEASE  PETITION + RELEASE + ANCHOR
You ask healing to flow into the target and release completely in a single moment rather than persisting. The ANCHOR remains — the healing takes root — but the BIND is gone, so the effect cannot be sustained or built upon. Heals 10 HP instantly. Cannot be maintained or extended.
Instantaneous rather than sustained. A useful emergency application when there is no time to maintain contact, but the loss of BIND means the healing cannot be extended beyond the single cast.
Variation III — Replace ANCHOR with SHIFT  PETITION + ANCHOR
You ask healing to anchor in the target rather than completing itself and releasing. Heals 10 HP and grants the target +1 temporary damage reduction as the body's restored conviction holds against further injury.
Adaptive range — the healing finds its own targets within reach. The loss of ANCHOR means the restoration does not take root as firmly; it heals what it finds rather than deeply restoring a specific site. Less effective for critical single-target healing, more effective when multiple allies are wounded.

PART FIVE: OUTCOMES OF MODIFIED CASTING
A modification attempted at the Expert rank carries real uncertainty. The configuration is unfamiliar — the mage has not yet built the repetitions that make a somatic structure automatic. Precision and quality of execution determine the outcome, and those qualities vary with the mage's depth of understanding of what they have chosen to do.
I have observed five distinct outcomes in mages attempting modifications for the first time. They form a spectrum from exceptional coherence through full coherence, partial resolution, collapse, and backfire. Understanding them in advance prepares the mage for what they will encounter — and more importantly, explains why each outcome takes the form it does.


Exceptional coherence: a rare outcome, and the one most practitioners describe as the moment they truly understood what they had been learning. The spell does not merely work — it reveals something about the component structure that the mage had not consciously understood before casting. The configuration feels completely natural, as though the mage had been casting it for years. This outcome typically accelerates the mage's development with the new configuration: the same swap will feel significantly easier on subsequent attempts.Full coherence: the modification works cleanly. The spell takes the new configuration without resistance. The mage will recognize this immediately — there is a quality of settledness to it, the components fitting together in a way that feels, despite being unfamiliar, correct. This is the outcome available to a mage who has genuinely understood both what they are removing and what they are adding.
Partial resolution: the modification works, but the new component's nature produces a consequence the mage did not fully anticipate. The spell does what was intended, and something else as well — something that follows directly from the component that was introduced. STABILIZE introduced into Fireball produces a persistent burning field, and it also holds the mage in a sustained argument with entropy that proves harder to maintain than expected. BIND introduced into Invisibility produces a concealment that holds unusually firm, including against the mage's own attempts to end it. These outcomes are coherent. The mage who understands the components will recognise, in hindsight, why they occurred. The surprise comes from insufficient attention to what the new component actually is, not from the magic behaving arbitrarily.
Collapse: the modification fizzles. The spell forms and then loses coherence before it can complete. The mage will feel this as a specific kind of physical strain — the effort of holding a configuration that the body and will have not yet made automatic, released without result. The strain is real. Three points of irreducible damage is the cost documented across the practitioners I have consulted on this question; the experience is described uniformly as a sudden release of held tension, followed by fatigue disproportionate to the effort expended. The modification was coherent. The execution was insufficient.
Backfire: the most instructive outcome, and the most dangerous. When a modification backfires, the spell does manifest — but in a form governed by the absence of the removed component rather than the presence of the new one. The removed component's gap becomes the dominant fact of the spell's behavior. Soul Trap attempted with BIND replaced produces a severance without a housing — the soul is cut from its transit path and then finds the nearest available vessel, which may be the caster. Fireball attempted with PROJECT replaced produces an explosion without reach — the concentrated force has nowhere to go and detonates at the point of origin. In each case, the backfire reveals exactly what the removed component was doing, by demonstrating what happens when it is absent. A mage who survives a backfire and understands its logic has learned something genuine about the spell's structure. The cost is five points of damage and the consequences of whatever the spell does in its distorted form.












A mage at Master rank modifies without uncertainty. The fluency they have built across years of practice extends to new configurations — the somatic structure settles automatically, the way a sentence settles for someone who has spoken a language long enough that grammar requires no conscious attention. Complicated outcomes and unexpected secondary effects remain possible, because magic rewards attention at every level of practice. What falls away at Master rank is the possibility of collapse or backfire arising from the modification itself.

CLOSING REMARKS
I have described the somatic components in as much detail as I am capable of providing in written form. There is a limit to what can be communicated this way, and I am aware of it. The components are not ideas about magic. They are descriptions of relationships that exist in the act of casting. Reading about ANCHOR is not the same as feeling ANCHOR operate in your own practice. No text can substitute for that.
What this text can do is give you the vocabulary to pay attention to what you are already doing. When you cast Oakflesh after reading this, you will feel something you may not have consciously noticed before — the particular quality of a spell that is rooted in the caster rather than projected from them. That is ANCHOR. You have been doing it every time. I have simply told you what it is.
The same is true of every component. They were always operating. This text has not changed your magic. It has given you a way to see it.
What you do with that seeing is yours. The modifications I have described are examples. They do not exhaust the possibilities. Two mages who have read this text and are standing in the same situation with the same spell may arrive at entirely different modifications based on what they need in that moment. This is correct. The components are a grammar, not a vocabulary list. A grammar enables sentences that have never been spoken before.
Speak carefully. Speak with conviction. Understand what you are saying before you say it. That is all I have to teach.
