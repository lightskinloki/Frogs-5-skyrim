PARTITION TWO
On the Distinction Between Combat Frequency and Atmospheric Frequency
 
The Primer established a framework that this volume must now refine, because students who have internalized the Primer's approach sometimes bring incorrect assumptions to the atmospheric techniques. The distinction is worth stating explicitly before we proceed to theory.
The Primer's songs operate through force. The Sunder-Chord projects an inverse frequency into matter and commands it to reject its own structural integrity. The Iron-Hymn creates a standing wave that enforces physical stability in your allies. The Dread-Call injects a psycho-acoustic dissonance into the cognitive processes of your enemies. In all three cases, you are applying acoustic force to a target and compelling a result. The physical world yields because you are playing mathematics louder than it can resist.
The atmospheric techniques do not operate through force. They operate through attention and precision. The Resonant Survey does not compel the space around you to reveal its contents — it listens to what the space is already producing and interprets it. The Deflection Note does not force sound to travel a false path — it exploits the geometry of reflection that already governs how sound moves through the environment. The Compression Toll is the closest of the four to the Primer's approach, and even it works by shaping a pressure differential that the architecture of the space makes possible, not by bludgeoning the air into compliance.
This distinction has a practical consequence addressed in full in Partition Five. For now: the Primer's songs cost you effort in proportion to their force. The atmospheric techniques cost you effort in proportion to your precision. These are different costs, and students who do not understand this arrive at Partition Six expecting something simpler than what they find.
 


 
PARTITION THREE
The Theory of Atmospheric Resonance
 
The Primer established that reality is a Standing Wave — that matter is frequency calcified by the long-held notes of the Earth Bones. We accepted this as the foundation of the Tonal Arts and moved on to application. Volume II requires that we revisit it with more granularity, focused not on solid matter but on the medium through which all Tonal work must travel.
Air is not nothing. This is obvious but must be stated precisely: air is a dense physical medium composed of particulate matter, moisture, temperature gradients, and pressure zones. It has weight. It has resistance. It has, crucially, a memory. When a sound wave passes through air, the pressure changes propagate outward, reflect off surfaces, interfere with other pressure changes, and produce secondary effects that persist for measurable time after the original sound has ended. A room that has recently been occupied is acoustically different from an empty one. A corridor with a concealed alcove reflects sound differently than a smooth-walled passage. The medium retains the shape of what has moved through it.
 
Pressure and Density
Air pressure is not uniform. Temperature differentials, physical constrictions, moving bodies, and sustained sound waves all create local variations in pressure density. A narrow doorway creates a natural pressure differential between the spaces it connects. A fire in a hearth creates rising thermal columns that displace cooler air outward. A body moving through a corridor pushes a small pressure wave ahead of itself. A Tonal Architect who understands these variations can read them — and, with appropriate technique, reshape them.
 
Reflection and Geometry
Sound reflects off hard smooth surfaces and is absorbed by soft irregular ones. Stone walls are excellent reflectors. Tapestries and bodies are effective absorbers. A practitioner who understands the geometry of a space can predict with reasonable accuracy where a projected frequency will travel, where it will reflect, and where it will arrive. This is the foundation of both the Deflection Note and the Directed Amplification. Diffraction — the bending of sound waves around obstacles — is equally important. Sound does not travel in straight lines through complex environments. It bends around corners, leaks through gaps, and arrives at locations with no direct acoustic line-of-sight. The Field Practicum exercises are designed in part to develop the student's practical feel for where these properties apply and where they fail.
 


 
PARTITION FOUR
On Technique and Instrument
 
The Primer assumed the war horn because the Primer was written for battlefields. This volume is not. Before addressing the specific techniques, the question of instrument must be settled.
 
The War Horn
The war horn remains viable for atmospheric work, particularly for the Compression Toll, which requires sustained volume to generate meaningful pressure differentials in a bounded space. Its disadvantage is subtlety — or rather the complete absence of it. A war horn played in a narrow corridor announces the presence of the player to everyone in range. This is sometimes acceptable. It is sometimes fatal. The student makes this assessment based on circumstances.
 
The Voice
A trained voice is a more precise instrument than the horn for most atmospheric applications. It offers finer pitch control, real-time frequency modulation, and considerably more discretion. The Resonant Survey and the Deflection Note can both be performed effectively with a sustained hummed tone, inaudible at the distances where their effects are most useful. Students who have trained only with the horn are advised to spend time developing a sustained resonant chest tone — not performance volume, but the low drone produced by a slow exhale across a narrow aperture.
 
The Principle
The technique drives the instrument choice, not the reverse. Ask first what the technique requires — volume, precision, a sustained tone, a sharp attack, a narrow frequency range — and then select the instrument that best delivers it. Each technique in Partition Six notes the recommended instrument and the reason. Students are expected to develop their own judgment about alternatives as their experience grows.
 


 
PARTITION FIVE
On the Nature of Dual Execution
 
The Primer's combat songs ask one thing of you at a time. The Sunder-Chord requires breath and force — the physical output is the technique. Either the frequency meets the target at the correct pitch and reality yields, or it does not. This is appropriate for battlefield application. Under combat conditions, simplicity of execution is a virtue.
The atmospheric techniques are not simple. Each of them requires two distinct physical and mental processes to occur simultaneously and in correct proportion to one another. This is not a design choice on the part of the curriculum. It is a consequence of what the techniques are actually doing.
Consider the Resonant Survey. To perform it correctly, the practitioner must project a sustained low-frequency tone into the space ahead — a physical act requiring breath control, correct pitch, and sustained output. At the same moment, the practitioner must listen to the returning acoustic data and interpret it — an analytical act requiring concentration, pattern recognition, and the ability to distinguish meaningful reflection from ambient noise. These two demands are in direct conflict. Projecting sound requires the practitioner to produce output. Interpreting sound requires the practitioner to receive input. Holding both open simultaneously, without one collapsing the other, is the skill the Survey is actually teaching.
The same duality runs through every technique in this volume, though the specific pairing differs. The Compression Toll pairs raw physical output — the lung capacity and diaphragm discipline to sustain a drone at sufficient volume to build pressure — with the mental precision to hold the exact frequency required without drift. The Deflection Note pairs the physical act of aiming a frequency at the precise angle a surface demands with the spatial and tactical awareness to know where the reflection must arrive and whether it has done so correctly.
What this means in practice is that a partially executed technique is not merely a weakened version of a successful one. It is a different kind of outcome entirely, and a more dangerous one in specific ways. A practitioner who projects the Resonant Survey at the correct frequency but cannot attend to the return has spent breath and time and received nothing. A practitioner who attends carefully to the return but projected an imprecise tone is interpreting distorted data — and a false acoustic picture of the space ahead is worse than no picture at all if you act on it.
The partial failures of each technique are described specifically in Partition Six, under each note's entry. Read those descriptions. Knowing how a technique fails when only half of its execution succeeds is the most direct route to understanding what you are actually producing when both halves succeed in concert. The moment that coordination becomes natural — the moment the output and the listening cease to feel like separate acts — is the moment the technique has been truly learned.
 


 
PARTITION SIX
The Four Atmospheric Notes
 
The following four techniques constitute the full curriculum of Volume II. They cost 0 FP. They use breath, precision, and attention. They are presented in order of increasing physical demand.
Each technique entry describes three possible outcomes: full execution, partial execution, and failure. Partial execution occurs when the physical component of the technique is performed correctly but the analytical component is not, or vice versa. The specific nature of the compromise depends on which half failed, and this is noted individually for each technique.
 
Note One: The Resonant Survey
Theoretical Basis: Every enclosed space has a natural acoustic signature determined by its dimensions, surface materials, and current contents. A sustained low-frequency tone projected into that space reflects and diffracts according to the space's geometry. Living creatures generate their own low-frequency signature — the rhythm of breathing, the faint thrum of a heartbeat, the micro-vibration of a body held very still. By projecting a baseline frequency and listening to the returning data with trained attention, the practitioner constructs an approximate acoustic map of the space: its rough dimensions, significant contents, and occupied areas not visible from the practitioner's current position.
 
Mechanic:
◦       Action: Minor Action to begin. Sustained concentration to maintain (recommended maximum: two minutes continuous).
◦       Instrument: Sustained low hummed tone (voice preferred for discretion). War horn viable but loud.
◦       Full Execution: The practitioner detects the presence and general area of living creatures within 60 feet in an enclosed or semi-enclosed space. Hidden creatures are detectable. Significant structural features — hollow spaces, concealed doors, large voids — are detectable with an accurate read.
◦       Partial Execution: The frequency is projected correctly but the practitioner cannot cleanly attend to the return — or the practitioner attends carefully but the projected tone was imprecise, distorting the data. In either case the picture is incomplete. Something is present in the space, but the practitioner cannot determine what, how many, or where with confidence. Acting on partial Survey data is the practitioner's own risk.
◦       Failure: The practitioner produces a sound but receives no usable acoustic data. In hostile environments, the noise of the attempt may itself alert nearby occupants.
◦       Cannot penetrate: Running water, ambient noise above conversational threshold, heavily absorptive surfaces such as packed earth or dense undergrowth.
◦       Limitation: Cannot be read effectively while combat is underway. This is a before-or-after tool.
 
Note Two: The Deflection Note
Theoretical Basis: Sound reflects off smooth hard surfaces at an angle equal to its angle of incidence. A practitioner who understands this geometry can aim their frequency at a surface rather than a target, causing the sound to travel a bent path and appear to originate from the reflection point rather than the source. No illusion is involved. The air is doing what air does when a frequency meets a hard surface at the correct angle.
 
Mechanic:
◦       Action: Minor Action.
◦       Instrument: Voice or war horn.
◦       Requirement: A solid smooth reflecting surface (stone wall, ceiling, large shield, cliff face) within 30 feet.
◦       Full Execution: The sound appears to originate from the reflection point. Creatures in the area must satisfy themselves that the source is elsewhere — a task made considerably harder by the fact that it is not.
◦       Partial Execution: The physical angle is correct but the pitch drifts, or the pitch is exact but the angle is misjudged. The sound arrives near the intended area but not at the precise intended point. Whether this is convincing enough to deceive depends entirely on the circumstances and what the listener was already expecting to hear.
◦       Failure: The frequency misses the surface geometry entirely and disperses without meaningful reflection. The attempt is audible to anyone nearby as a poorly aimed horn blast or misdirected vocal noise with no obvious point of origin — which may itself draw attention.
◦       Limitation: Curved, irregular, or soft surfaces degrade reflection geometry significantly. Does not deceive a target that is already tracking the practitioner's position by sound.
 
Note Three: The Directed Amplification
Theoretical Basis: Sound in open air disperses in all directions, losing intensity with distance. By controlling breath pressure and horn geometry, a practitioner can project frequency in a narrow focused beam — high intensity along the axis, rapid dispersal to either side. From the beam's endpoint, a sound arrives at full volume. From anywhere not on that axis, the same sound is inaudible. This has two applications: signal delivery to a specific location without broadcasting, and targeted acoustic disruption at a single recipient.
 
Mechanic:
◦       Action: Major Action.
◦       Instrument: Voice sufficient for signal use. War horn required for disruption.
◦       Range: 60 feet (signal). 40 feet (disruption).
◦       Signal Use: A sound — a word, a tone, a signal — is delivered to a specific point. Inaudible to anyone not on the beam axis. Allies can be signalled without alerting nearby enemies.
◦       Disruption Use: A concentrated acoustic burst delivered at a frequency the target's processing cannot cleanly handle. If it lands correctly, the target's attention is fractured — their next action requiring focused concentration becomes considerably harder to execute cleanly.
◦       Partial Execution: The beam is physically produced but the mental geometry drifts, or the geometry is exact but the physical output loses focus. In signal use, the sound arrives audible to an unintended party as well as the intended one. In disruption use, the burst arrives diffuse rather than concentrated — present but not at the intensity required to interrupt cleanly.
◦       Failure: The beam disperses before reaching the target. In signal use, the message does not arrive or arrives unintelligibly. In disruption use, the technique produces no effect on the target.
◦       Limitation: Requires clear acoustic line-of-sight. Dense absorptive materials block the beam. The practitioner must know precisely where they are aiming.
 
Note Four: The Compression Toll
Theoretical Basis: A sustained low-frequency tone projected into a physically bounded space creates a zone of pressurized air that resists the passage of physical bodies. The resistance is proportional to the degree of constriction: the narrower and more enclosed the passage, the more efficiently the pressure differential builds. The effect is barely audible — a subsonic thrum felt in the chest rather than heard with the ears. This is the most physically demanding technique in this volume and the only one with direct tactical utility equivalent to the Primer's combat songs. It is not a weapon. It is a gate.
 
Mechanic:
◦       Action: Major Action to start. Major Action to maintain each subsequent round — identical to the Primer's song sustain rule.
◦       Instrument: War horn required. This technique demands sustained volume the voice cannot reliably produce.
◦       Requirement: A physically bounded space no wider than 15 feet, with solid surfaces on at least two sides.
◦       Full Execution: The zone is established at full pressure. Movement through it is actively resisted — those attempting to cross find the air itself working against their forward motion.
◦       Partial Execution: The breath is sufficient to establish a pressure zone but the frequency drifts without the mental discipline to hold it precisely — or the frequency is exact but the breath column cannot sustain full pressure. The zone is present and detectable but reduced. Movement through it is slowed and uncomfortable rather than actively resisted.
◦       Failure: The breath is insufficient to build meaningful pressure at the required frequency, or the frequency is lost before pressure builds. No zone is established. The horn blast is audible to everyone in range.
◦       Secondary: The subsonic thrum of a successfully maintained Toll is detectable to any creature with acute hearing. It cannot be silenced.
◦       Limitation: Requires continuous play — the effect ends the moment the practitioner stops. Has no meaningful effect in open environments without physical boundaries to build pressure against.
 


 
PARTITION SEVEN
Field Limitations and Environmental Conditions
 
All four atmospheric techniques depend on the physical behavior of air in real environments. Real environments are not ideal. The following conditions should be understood before deployment.
 
Optimal Environments
All four techniques perform best in stone-walled enclosed spaces: dungeons, ruins, fortresses, mines, cave systems. Hard smooth reflective surfaces amplify the acoustic precision these techniques require. The Compression Toll is most effective in chokepoints of the right width — too narrow and pressure bleeds back on the practitioner; too wide and it disperses before building. Well-preserved Dwemer ruins deserve special mention: the precision-machined bronze surfaces of Dwemer architecture create near-perfect acoustic reflection and often carry residual pressure signatures from the ruins' original atmospheric systems. A practitioner working in a Dwemer facility is working on a surface that was built for this purpose.
 
Poor Environments
Open outdoor environments are uniformly poor for atmospheric work. Forests are worst — irregular absorptive surfaces scatter sound unpredictably and prevent the pressure differentials the Compression Toll requires. Swamps introduce moisture gradients that distort acoustic travel. Open plains allow for signal use of the Directed Amplification at distance but are useless for the Survey and the Toll. Wind is the practitioner's primary environmental adversary. A moderate crosswind will deflect directed frequencies enough to invalidate the Deflection Note's geometry and scatter the Directed Amplification's beam. Wind speed and direction must be assessed before outdoor use.
 
Against Acoustically Unusual Targets
All four techniques depend on the physical movement of air and do not require the target to consciously process sound. A deaf creature is still spatially detectable by the Survey, still displaced by the Compression Toll, and still deceived by the Deflection Note provided it has functional spatial orientation. However, a creature with no functional inner ear — certain constructs, certain undead — will be unaffected by the Directed Amplification's disruption use, which requires an auditory processing system to interrupt. Know your target before committing to a technique.
 


 
PARTITION EIGHT
Safety Protocols
 
The primary physical risks of atmospheric technique are cumulative rather than acute. You will not immediately injure yourself performing a Deflection Note. You will, over time, damage your hearing and your larynx if you ignore the protocols below.
 
The Standard Warning Signs
Any practitioner who experiences the following during or after performance should stop immediately, consume food and water, and seek assistance: a persistent metallic taste, a cold sensation in the extremities inconsistent with the ambient temperature, blurred peripheral vision, or colored rings around light sources. These signs are universal across all Tonal techniques and indicate that the body's physical tolerance for acoustic performance is approaching its limit.
 
Extended Resonant Survey
Sustained Survey beyond five minutes of continuous use may produce auditory confusion as the brain begins interpreting ambient atmospheric noise as significant acoustic data. These effects resolve within minutes of ending the technique. Acting on that confusion in a hostile environment is the actual danger. Two minutes maximum continuous use, equal rest before resuming.
 
Compression Toll Feedback
If the bounded space is too small, pressure buildup will reflect back on the practitioner, manifesting as resistance in the chest, difficulty sustaining the breath column, and progressive tinnitus. If these appear during the Toll, end the technique and move away immediately. Pushing through feedback will not intensify the effect on those attempting to cross. It will injure the practitioner.
 
Voice Use
Students who use their voice as the primary instrument should be aware that the sustained frequencies required sit outside the normal vocal range and place significant strain on the larynx over time. Rest between applications is mandatory. Using the voice for multiple atmospheric techniques combined with the Primer's combat songs in the same encounter risks temporary degradation of vocal range severe enough to impair the combat songs as well. Rest the voice. It is not expendable.
 


 
PARTITION NINE
The Dwemer Pipe Organs: A Historical Note
 
The Primer noted that the techniques it described were crude biological approximations of Dwemer Tonal Resonators — that the underlying physics are identical whether the note is produced by a steam valve or a human lung. This observation is worth extending for the atmospheric context specifically, because the Dwemer application of atmospheric manipulation illustrates the outer limit of what this discipline can achieve.
The brass pipe systems found throughout Dwemer ruins have been described by surface scholars as industrial plumbing or waste systems. They are not. Examined with acoustic attention, they are organs — instruments designed to maintain specific atmospheric pressure signatures throughout a defined space, sustaining the precise conditions the Dwemer required for habitation and work at depth.
The mechanism that kept air breathable in the deep Dwemer cities was not magical filtration. It was acoustic. The Dwemer sustained atmospheric resonance signatures that produced the same effect as a natural chimney draw — moving stale air out and fresh air through — without a single enchantment. Pure applied physics, run at mechanical precision, sustained indefinitely by instruments that did not require lungs or attention.
The student performing the Resonant Survey in a Dwemer ruin is projecting frequency into a space that was designed for this purpose and has been tuned by centuries of acoustic use. Pay attention to how the space responds differently than a Nordic ruin of equivalent size. That difference is information about the Dwemer's understanding of atmospheric physics that we have not yet fully recovered.
I mention the Dwemer here not as historical background but as practical context. The work that follows Volume II will take you into a Dwemer facility. The atmospheric techniques are not preparation for that work in a vague sense. They are specific prerequisites. The Dean does not assign curricula that are not applicable.

Experiment Two: The Survey Prediction Test

A FINAL NOTE
 
Dear Student,
 
Three songs. That is more than most people who call themselves Bards accumulate in a career of performing for silver in cold inns. It is also, in the broader context of what the Tonal Arts can become, approximately the beginning of the beginning.
What this volume asks of you is different from what the Primer asked. The combat songs are force. They are legible. You play them and something in the physical world visibly yields. The atmospheric techniques are not like that. The Resonant Survey tells you something is there before you can see it. The Deflection Note puts a sound somewhere it did not come from. The Compression Toll turns a doorway into a wall without touching it. These are not impressive in the way a Sunder-Chord is impressive. They are impressive in the way a well-placed question is impressive — specifically, when nothing else would have gotten you the same result.
Partition Five describes the principle of Dual Execution. Read it again before you attempt anything in the field. The partial failure outcomes described under each technique in Partition Six are not there to discourage you. They are there because knowing exactly how a technique degrades when only half of its execution succeeds is the most direct route to understanding what you are producing when both halves hold together. The moment that coordination becomes instinctive — the moment the output and the listening cease to feel like separate demands — is the moment you will understand what this volume was trying to teach.
Complete the Practicum honestly. Write what happened, not what you hoped would happen. The gap between acoustic theory and acoustic reality is always instructive and always specific to the space you were standing in. That specificity is the education. Bring the completed log when you return to Solitude. Bring also any notes on spaces that behaved unexpectedly — anything the text would not have predicted. That information will be relevant to what comes next.
Travel carefully.

I. THE CORE MECHANIC: DUAL EXECUTION
In Volume II, you are doing two things at once: physically projecting sound (Might or Agility) and mentally interpreting or focusing it (Guile). This requires rolling two d20s—one for each attribute.
Determining the Five Outcomes for Combined Rolls:
1. CRITICAL SUCCESS (The Masterpiece):
   * Trigger: Both dice pass, and at least one of them is a Natural 1.
   * Result: You achieve the full effect plus a significant tactical advantage (see individual Note descriptions).
2. 3. CLEAN SUCCESS (Professional Execution):
   * Trigger: Both dice pass (under or equal to your Stat Scores), but neither is a 1.
   * Result: The Note works exactly as intended.
4. 5. SUCCESS WITH A COMPLICATION (The Strained Note):
   * Trigger: Only one die passes.
   * Result: You achieve the effect, but with a physical or tactical cost. The "Partial Execution" descriptions in the Treatise provide the details.
6. 7. SIMPLE FAILURE (The Whiff):
   * Trigger: Both dice fail (over your Stat Scores), but neither is a 20.
   * Result: You spend your breath and make noise, but the atmosphere does not respond.
8. 9. CRITICAL FAILURE (The Collapse):
   * Trigger: At least one die is a Natural 20.
   * Result: The frequency reflects back on you. You take 2 Irreducible Damage, lose your next turn to a coughing fit/vertigo, and alert all nearby enemies.
