Chapter I: The Mythological Landscape of Tamriel and the Question of Divine Heritage
The Elven Paradigm of Divine Descent
To understand the revolutionary nature of the draconic theory regarding Nordic origins, we must first establish the existing paradigm of divine heritage as understood through Elven mythology and accepted historical scholarship. The elves of Tamriel have, since time immemorial, maintained their connection to divinity through a complex but well-documented creation mythology that has become the foundation of academic understanding regarding the relationship between mortal and divine.
The grand creation story of the elves speaks of their tragic decline from divinity, a narrative that has become so deeply embedded in scholarly consciousness that it is rarely questioned despite its extraordinary claims. The reputation of the trickster Lorkhan has been immortalized in the very landscape of Morrowind, where Red Mountain stands as a hulking gray rupture in Nirn's crust, spewing what the Dunmer call "his lies as choking ash and his false promises as molten rock."
This geological monument to divine betrayal represents more than mere symbolism. Red Mountain serves as physical proof of the supernatural events described in Elven mythology, lending credence to their claims of divine heritage. The mountain emerged when Auriel fired Lorkhan's heart into the sea, an event that scholars accept as historical fact despite its obviously supernatural nature. This acceptance reveals a fundamental bias in our approach to mythological claims that becomes crucial when examining Nordic legends.
The elves' status as natives to Tamriel, having arrived on what they call "Dawn's Beauty" unscathed while the wandering men were scattered on foreign lands, forms the foundation of their claim to legitimacy and divine favor. The sundering of Old Ehlnofey is understood not as a literal geographical event, but as a metaphor for their decline from divine status to mortal form. Yet this metaphorical interpretation does not diminish the accepted reality of their divine origins.
The elves are acknowledged as Auriel's disciples, descendants of the Aedra whose glorious heritage is integral to their identity. Their decline from immortal divine beings to mortal elves is documented, accepted, and forms the basis of their religious and political systems. The weakening Aedra gave up their power to provide structure to the world in the form of the Earth Bones, a sacrifice that is understood as both literal and metaphysical truth.
The Human Condition and Perceived Divine Subordination
In stark contrast to the elves' accepted divine heritage, the human races of Tamriel appear to occupy a fundamentally different position in relation to the divine. The wandering Ehlnofey, ancestors to humanity, share similar roots to the elves in the old Ehlnofey, yet their relationship with the divine realm is characterized by subservience rather than kinship.
Lorkhan, known to the human races as Shezarr or Shor depending upon cultural variation, is revered not as ancestor but as divine patron. He is honored for conceptualizing the mortal realm and facilitating the birth of mortality itself, but this reverence maintains a clear distinction between worshiper and deity. Similarly, men kneel as lesser beings before Akatosh and the other Divines, their prayers and offerings acknowledging their inferior status in the cosmic hierarchy.
This apparent disparity in divine relationship raises profound theological and historical questions that have been insufficiently explored by mainstream scholarship. If the elves can legitimately claim kinship with Auriel, tracing their lineage directly back to the divine Aedra who sacrificed themselves to create Mundus, then what prevents men from claiming similar kinship with Akatosh? The logical consistency of our cosmological understanding demands that either both claims are valid or both are suspect.
The human races have indeed known wandering, scattered across foreign lands before their various migrations to Tamriel. Yet this scattering, rather than being seen as evidence of divine disfavor, might instead represent a different form of divine purpose. Where the elves remained in one place and declined, humans were distributed across multiple continents, suggesting a broader cosmic role that has yet to be fully understood.
The Obscured Histories of Other Continents
The mystery deepening our ignorance of human divine heritage lies in the fundamentally different nature of documentation available for the various continents of Nirn. While the events unfolding on Tamriel during the early Merethic Era are clear and well-documented, with extensive knowledge of the diverging groups of Aldmer as they came to occupy their corners of the continent, the ancient histories of Akavir, Yokuda, and most importantly Atmora remain largely unknown to contemporary scholarship.
This disparity in available historical documentation is not merely inconvenient but actively misleading. Our understanding of the relationship between mortal and divine, between human and Aedra, has been shaped almost entirely by sources originating from Tamriel itself. The perspectives of other continents, other human cultures, and other relationships with divinity have been lost to time, distance, or deliberate obscuration.
Atmora, the fatherland of the Nords, represents perhaps the greatest gap in our historical knowledge. What we know of Atmoran culture comes primarily from Nordic sources recorded centuries or millennia after the migration to Skyrim. The original Atmoran perspective on divinity, on the nature of their gods, and on their own cosmic role has been filtered through generations of retelling, cultural adaptation, and potential deliberate revision.
The dragons themselves, as we shall explore in detail, originated from the distant north of Atmora according to scholarly sources. The relationship between dragons and early Atmoran culture was not merely one of worship but of direct rule, with dragons serving as god-kings and Atmoran humans as their devoted subjects. Yet the nature of this relationship, its origins, and its ultimate transformation remain mysterious due to our lack of direct Atmoran sources.
The Nordic Claim to Divine Uniqueness
The Nords themselves have always claimed to be fundamentally different from other mortal races, a claim that mainstream scholarship has typically dismissed as cultural chauvinism rather than examined as potential historical truth. They call themselves "the children of the sky," a designation that implies direct divine parentage rather than mere divine favor. They refer to Skyrim as "the throat of the world" because, according to their traditions, it is where the sky exhaled on the land and formed them.
This creation myth deserves more serious consideration than it has received from academic sources. The Nords see themselves as eternal outsiders and invaders, maintaining this identity even when they conquer and rule other peoples. They feel no kinship with other mortal races, suggesting a fundamental difference in origin or nature that transcends mere cultural preference.
The breath and voice as the vital essence of a Nord is commonly dismissed by scholars as simple reference to their use of the Storm Voice, the Thu'um that allows them to speak with draconic power. Yet this interpretation may be superficial, missing deeper implications about the fundamental nature of Nordic being. If breath and voice are truly the vital essence of a Nord, then their connection to the dragons who also speak reality into existence through vocalization becomes more than coincidental.
When Nords speak of being formed by the sky's exhalation, they may be describing a literal rather than metaphorical process of creation. The sky, associated with Kyne and ultimately with the divine realm of Aetherius, breathing life into human forms could represent an alternative creation myth that places Nordic origins in direct divine action rather than evolutionary development from Ehlnofey ancestors.
The Double Standard in Historical Interpretation
The scholarly community demonstrates a pronounced double standard when evaluating supernatural claims in historical sources. Elven mythology, with its accounts of divine sacrifice, geographical transformation through divine action, and the literal embodiment of gods in physical form, is treated as historically accurate despite the obviously supernatural nature of these claims.
We accept without question that the weakening Aedra gave up their power to provide structure to the world in the form of the Earth Bones. We accept that Auriel fired Lorkhan's heart into the sea, causing Red Mountain to emerge from beneath the waves. We acknowledge that the Chimer were transformed into Dunmer through direct divine intervention following the actions of the Tribunal. These supernatural events are treated as established historical fact.
Yet when similar supernatural elements appear in Nordic historical accounts, they are dismissed as folklore, embellishment, or symbolic representation rather than literal truth. The songs of the return, which document Ysgramor's legendary exploits, are treated as entertainment rather than historical record despite containing detailed accounts of specific events, locations, and individuals.
This interpretive bias reveals underlying assumptions about which cultures have access to genuine divine intervention and which do not. The elves are granted the benefit of believing their supernatural claims because they fit within established academic paradigms about divine heritage and cosmic significance. Human claims to supernatural origins or abilities are dismissed because they challenge the assumed hierarchy that places elves closer to divinity than men.
The consequences of this double standard extend beyond mere academic preference. By dismissing Nordic supernatural claims while accepting Elven ones, we may have overlooked crucial evidence about the true nature of human origins, divine relationships, and cosmic purpose. The very evidence that could revolutionize our understanding of Tamrielic history may have been categorized as myth rather than examined as potential historical truth.

Unlike the literate Elven cultures with their extensive written records, early Nordic culture relied heavily upon oral tradition for the preservation of historical knowledge. The skalds, professional storytellers and historians, bore the responsibility for maintaining accurate historical records through memorized verse and song.
This oral tradition should not be dismissed as inherently less reliable than written records. Cultures that depend upon oral preservation of crucial information develop sophisticated mnemonic techniques and social structures to ensure accuracy. The skalds underwent extensive training to memorize vast amounts of information with precise accuracy, and their social status depended upon their ability to recite historical accounts correctly.
The Songs of the Return represent the pinnacle of this oral historical tradition. These are not casual folk tales or entertainment pieces, but formal historical records preserved in verse form for easier memorization and transmission. The songs contain specific details about individuals, relationships, locations, and events that demonstrate their function as genuine historical documentation rather than fictional narrative.
The poetic structure of the songs serves a practical purpose in oral culture while also elevating the content to sacred status. The rhythmic and melodic elements aid memorization, while the elevated language indicates the cosmic significance of the events being described. When the songs describe supernatural events, they do so with the same detailed specificity used for mundane historical facts, suggesting that the supernatural elements were understood as equally real and historically accurate.
